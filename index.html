Eleftheria Briakou

Ph.D. Candidate

University of Maryland, College Park

I am a fourth-year Ph.D. Candidate in the Department of Computer Science at the University of Maryland, College Park. I am a member of the CLIP lab advised by Marine Carpuat. My research interests are in Multilingual Natural Language Processing and Machine Translation. My recent work focuses on detecting and analyzing fine-grained semantic divergences—fine meaning differences in texts that overlap in content—in parallel texts to improve machine and human translation understanding.

Previously, I interned at Google Translate with Colin Cherry and George Foster (Summer 2022), at Facebook AI with Marjan Ghazvininejad and Luke Zettlemoyer (Summer 2021) and at Dataminr with Joel Tetreault (Summer 2020).

February 10, 2022 9:00 AM Online

Tracking progress in Style Transfer: From Human to Automatic Evaluation.

While the field of style transfer (ST) has been growing rapidly, progress has been hampered by a lack of standardized practices for both human and automatic evaluation. In this talk, we will first summarize human evaluation practices described in 97 style transfer papers with respect to three main evaluation aspects: style transfer, meaning preservation, and fluency. As we will see, protocols for human evaluations in ST are often underspecified and not standardized, which hampers the reproducibility of research in this field and progress toward better human and automatic evaluation methods. Then, we will switch gears and discuss issues in automatic evaluation of ST. Concretely, taking formality as a case study, we will revisit several metrics for automatic evaluation of each of the three ST aspects and finally outline best practices that correlate well with human judgments and are robust across languages.

October 20, 2021 10:00 AM Online

How do Cross-lingual Semantic Divergences Impact Neural Machine Translation?

While it has been shown that Neural Machine Translation (NMT) is highly sensitive to noisy parallel training samples, prior work treats all types of mismatches between source and target as noise. As a result, it remains unclear how samples that are mostly equivalent but contain a small number of semantically divergent tokens impact NMT training. In this talk, we will first discuss how such fine-grained semantic divergences can be detected without supervision. Then, we will analyze their impact on Transformer models, and, finally, we will discuss how they can be integrated into Transformers as factors.
